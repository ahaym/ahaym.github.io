Introducing .tql

A markdown-shaped semantic layer for the agentic era — and a short history of why every previous attempt looked like YAML.

TextQL Engineering · Reference Article · Ontology Repo Series

A .tql file is the modeling primitive that TextQL uses to govern metrics, dimensions, joins, filters, and access logic inside an ontology repo. It compiles to warehouse-native SQL. It looks, on purpose, more like a markdown note than a YAML config.1 It is, also on purpose, one more domain-specific language in a long line of domain-specific languages that have tried to do roughly the same job. The bet is that this one is built for a different reader.

Every previous attempt (LookML, MetricFlow, OmniML, Cube's yml, Malloy) was designed for an organization of humans curating a small, MECE catalog of definitions. .tql is designed for an organization where a fleet of agents read millions of characters in seconds, write definitions after every user thread, and let usage canonize what "revenue" actually means.

This is the article-length version of why we built it, what it is, and what it isn't.

I — LineageFrom LookML to OmniML

The modern semantic layer begins in 2012, when Lloyd Tabb shipped LookML at Looker. The design move was small and obvious in retrospect: take traditional OLAP modeling (measures, dimensions, derived tables, joins) and express it in YAML so it could be version-controlled, code-reviewed, and shared across an organization.2

What LookML got right has been the implicit template for every semantic layer built since. There is one canonical definition of revenue, referenced everywhere. Joins and rollups live in a single file and are reused by reference. Metrics are MECE: mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive, with no two metrics secretly computing the same thing in different ways. The data team curates the catalog, and naming conflicts get resolved in a meeting. Every duplicated definition is debt.

It is a DRY worldview, and for a decade it was the right one. Humans are the bottleneck, humans don't scale, and so every byte of definition has to be precious, curated, and singular.

# metrics/revenue.lkml — the canonical shape
measure: total_revenue {
  type: sum
  sql: ${TABLE}.net_revenue ;;
  label: "Net Revenue"
  description: "GAAP-compliant net revenue. See data-gov doc #44."
}

In 2021, Benn Stancil published The missing piece of the modern data stack, which gave the pattern a name (the metrics layer) and a thesis: pull metric definitions out of every individual BI tool and put them in a shared, headless service that any consumer could hit.3 The thesis was correct; the execution went sideways. Within eighteen months, at least six companies were building something that could reasonably be called a metrics layer, and four years later the results looked like this:

The most successful direct descendant of LookML's design philosophy turned out, uncoincidentally, to be built by the people who originally built LookML. Omni was founded in 2022 by Colin Zima, Jamie Davidson, and Chris Merrick, all from Looker, and shipped OmniML: LookML's design philosophy with ergonomic improvements (incremental modeling, in-UI authoring, an MCP server). In April 2026, Omni raised $120M at a $1.51B valuation, pitched explicitly as the context layer for enterprise AI. Same shape as LookML, better paint.

Every company has its own definition of revenue, customer, and last quarter. Natural language only works at scale with calibrated context. Colin Zima, Omni

This is correct. The question is what shape that calibrated context should take, and who (or what) is supposed to write and read it.

II — Interlude"Let's try this one again, I guess."

That is the opening line of Benn Stancil's August 2025 retrospective, The context layer, and it is the cleanest articulation of where the discourse landed.4 Four years after he proposed the metrics layer, he wrote: It didn't work for us before… but it might work for us now.

The reason it didn't work was not technical, it was economic. A standalone semantic layer is hard to sell without a BI tool attached, as Fivetran's George Fraser predicted in 2021 and as the graveyard above confirmed. The reason it might work now is that the consumer of the semantic layer is changing: it used to be a BI tool with a human at the keyboard, and increasingly it is an agent.

A note on naming

Erik, if you're reading: it's not technically a query language. It's a modeling file. You're allowed to be mad anyway.5

So the consumer changed, which means the optimal shape of the artifact changed. Every semantic layer descended from LookML, including OmniML, MetricFlow, and the dbt Semantic Layer, was shaped for the wrong reader.

III — The shape problemWhat the YAML lineage assumes

Strip away the syntax differences and every LookML-descended semantic layer rests on the same five assumptions. New definitions are scarce and expensive, requiring a pull request, a review, and a name negotiation. Redundancy is the enemy; if two metrics overlap, one of them must die. Naming is canonical; there is exactly one revenue, and the governance committee decides which one. The reader is a human with a screen, scanning files and jumping between symbols. And curation precedes use: the model is finished before it is queried.

That somebody is often a data governance committee, and they meet on Thursdays.

All five assumptions are downstream of the bandwidth of a human reader. A human can hold roughly one screen of context in mind at a time. Redundancy is genuinely costly for a human, who has to choose which definition to trust; naming collisions genuinely confuse, because a human can't keep both meanings live simultaneously. LLMs invert every one of these constraints.

IV — What changedThe bitter lesson, applied to modeling files

A frontier model reads a few million characters of context in a few seconds, does not get bored, and is not confused by three slightly different definitions of active customer sitting in the same folder. It can hold all three live, notice that they agree on the load-bearing clause and disagree on the trailing predicate, and decide which one the user means.

This is the inversion. The properties LookML treats as virtues (DRY, MECE, one canonical definition per concept) are virtues only relative to a human reader. For an agent they are constraints that make the model harder to write, slower to iterate on, and less likely to reflect what the business currently means.

The properties LookML treats as vices (redundancy, overlap, multiple coexisting definitions, prose mixed with structure) are not vices for an agent. They carry signal. If revenue is defined in seventeen .tql files and fourteen of them compute SUM(net_revenue) while three compute SUM(gross_revenue), that is not a governance failure. It is the answer to the question what does this company usually mean by revenue?, expressed as a frequency distribution rather than a committee decision.

Don't try to canonize meaning through a data governance committee. Let usage canonize it, and let the agent read the usage.

This is the bitter lesson applied to data modeling.6 Stop trying to encode the right answer into a rigid hand-curated structure. Build the loosest structure that still parses, give the agent everything, and let frequency win.

The governance committee, mercifully, gets its Thursdays back.

V — What .tql actually isAs markdown-like as humanly possible

A .tql file is what you get if you sit down to design a semantic layer with two non-negotiable constraints. First, it must compile to inspectable, warehouse-native SQL with no black-box query compiler in between. Second, it must be as close to a markdown note as a parseable file can possibly be.

The same way LookML was YAML with measures and dimensions bolted on, .tql is prose with SQL fragments bolted in. Parameters get a typed declaration block at the top. SQL goes between fragment delimiters. Everything else (helpers, lambdas, control flow, comments, sketches, half-finished thoughts) is allowed to be whatever it needs to be.

Here is what a real semantic-modeling file looks like: a single .tql file that accepts a list of metrics, a list of dimensions, a list of filters, and a sort order, and renders the right SQL for any combination of them.

-- queries/orders_explorer.tql
--
-- Generic explorer over the orders fact table. Pick metrics, dimensions,
-- filters, and a sort order; the file does the rest.
--
-- This is the version Sales asked for in the 2026-Q1 review. Revenue here
-- is whatever lives in orders.revenue (already pre-aggregated per order),
-- summed. Refunds are NOT subtracted, and test accounts are NOT excluded
-- — Sales wants the gross funnel including pilots. Finance uses a different
-- file for the board deck; see queries/revenue_finance_board.tql, which
-- subtracts refunds, excludes test accounts, and pulls from
-- analytics.orders_recognized instead of orders.
--
-- Known limitation: the customer_name filter path doesn't auto-pull the
-- customers join. If you filter on customer_name without also requesting
-- the customer dimension, the query will fail. We have not fixed this
-- because every caller in practice asks for both. Ana flagged it twice
-- in October; if she flags it a third time, we should wire the join in
-- through filterables instead of dim_entries.
--
-- Touch this file with care. It backs the Sales dashboard, three Slack
-- bots, and the Monday revenue digest.

params {
  metrics: Set<"revenue" | "order_count"> = []
  dimensions: Set<"customer" | "month"> = []
  filters: List<FilterInput> = []
  sort: List<"revenue desc" | "revenue asc" | "month asc" | "month desc"> = []
}

let
  metric_frags = matchSet metrics {
    "revenue"      -> sql"SUM(o.revenue) AS revenue"
    "order_count"  -> sql"COUNT(DISTINCT o.id) AS order_count"
  }
  dim_entries = matchSet dimensions {
    "customer"  -> { expr = sql"c.name", join = sql"JOIN customers c ON o.customer_id = c.id" }
    "month"     -> { expr = sql"DATE_TRUNC('month', o.ordered_at)", join = sql"" }
  }
  sort_frags = matchSet sort {
    "revenue desc"  -> sql"revenue DESC"
    "revenue asc"   -> sql"revenue ASC"
    "month desc"    -> sql"DATE_TRUNC('month', o.ordered_at) DESC"
    "month asc"     -> sql"DATE_TRUNC('month', o.ordered_at) ASC"
  }
  filterables = [
    { key = "customer_name", expr = sql"c.name",        ops = ["equals", "like"] }
  , { key = "ordered_at",    expr = sql"o.ordered_at",  ops = ["gte", "lte", "between"] }
  ]
  joins          = concatSep " "  (map (\d -> d.join) dim_entries)
  select_dims    = concatSep ", " (map (\d -> d.expr) dim_entries)
  select_metrics = concatSep ", " metric_frags
  select_expr =
    if isEmpty select_dims && isEmpty select_metrics then sql"*"
    else if isEmpty select_dims    then select_metrics
    else if isEmpty select_metrics then select_dims
    else sql"${select_dims}, ${select_metrics}"
  where_clause = wrap "WHERE "    "" (filterWhere filterables filters)
  group_clause = wrap "GROUP BY " "" select_dims
  order_clause = wrap "ORDER BY " "" (concatSep ", " sort_frags)
in sql''
  SELECT ${select_expr}
  FROM orders o
  ${joins}
  ${where_clause}
  ${group_clause}
  ${order_clause}
''
Called with this input
{
  "metrics":    ["revenue", "order_count"],
  "dimensions": ["customer", "month"],
  "filters": [
    { "key": "customer_name", "operator": "like", "value": "Acme" },
    { "key": "ordered_at",    "operator": "gte",  "value": "2026-01-01" }
  ],
  "sort": ["revenue desc", "month asc"]
}
Renders to this SQL
SELECT c.name, DATE_TRUNC('month', o.ordered_at),
       SUM(o.revenue) AS revenue,
       COUNT(DISTINCT o.id) AS order_count
FROM orders o
JOIN customers c ON o.customer_id = c.id
WHERE c.name ILIKE '%Acme%'
  AND o.ordered_at >= '2026-01-01'
GROUP BY c.name, DATE_TRUNC('month', o.ordered_at)
ORDER BY revenue DESC, DATE_TRUNC('month', o.ordered_at) ASC

That single file is doing the job most semantic layers spread across a measures.yml, a dimensions.yml, a joins config, a filter abstraction layer, and an ordering DSL. We could spend several paragraphs walking through what matchSet does, how the records-and-lambdas pattern lets dimensions bring their joins along, and why wrap is the right primitive for the conditional WHERE/GROUP BY/ORDER BY. We won't, because the point of this article is that you don't need that walkthrough and neither does any modern LLM. To make that concrete, here is what Claude did with the file when shown it cold, with no documentation, no system prompt, and no information about what .tql even is.

Given this file and no information about this language, can you back out the data modeling logic?  [file pasted]

Working backward from the query template, the data model is essentially a star schema with orders as the fact table.

Fact table: orders (aliased o)

The query is anchored on FROM orders o with no conditional logic around it, so orders is the grain: one row per order. From the columns referenced:

  • o.id — primary key, used with COUNT(DISTINCT) for order_count. The DISTINCT is a tell that the joins can fan out, which they don't here, so it's defensive — likely because additional joins (line items, payments) get added in other configurations of this template.
  • o.revenue — pre-aggregated monetary amount per order, summed for the revenue metric. Stored on the order itself rather than computed from line items, which suggests either denormalization for query speed or that line-item granularity isn't part of this model.
  • o.customer_id — foreign key to customers.
  • o.ordered_at — order timestamp, used both as a filterable range (gte/lte/between) and as the source for the month dimension via DATE_TRUNC. Stored as a timestamp, not pre-bucketed.
Dimension table: customers (aliased c)

Only joined in when the customer dimension or the customer_name filter is requested (well, the join is only added via dimensions in this snippet — the filter path would need the join wired in separately, which is a small gap in the template). Referenced columns:

  • c.id — primary key.
  • c.name — the customer's display name, used both as a group-by expression and as a filterable field with equals and like operators. like being supported implies free-text search over names.

That is not someone reading a generated SQL string. That is the model reverse-engineering the warehouse schema, identifying the grain, noticing that DISTINCT is defensive against join fan-out that doesn't happen here, and flagging a real bug in the template (the customer-name filter would fail because the join isn't wired in through the filter path). All from a file that we did not bother to document, in a language the model has never seen. The exercise that a conventional semantic layer puts the data team through, populating a catalog so the BI tool can reason about the schema, collapses into the model reading the file.

The mechanism, briefly

Three structural commitments keep the format from collapsing into chaos, and they are the only commitments .tql makes. Typed parameter blocks declare the public surface of a file; params { } lets an agent or a human inspect a file without rendering it and know exactly what it accepts. Trusted SQL fragments isolate the authored portion of the file from any caller-provided data, so that parameters interpolate as bind values and string-injection becomes a type error rather than a runtime concern. Files and folders are the only required organization, with no application database storing the "real" model; the repo is the model, git is the audit log, and access control is enforced at the file-tree level.

Everything else (the matching, the wrapping, the helper records, the filter machinery, the access control) is built out of these three primitives. There is no central compiler doing magic. An agent reading a .tql file sees what runs.7

VI — MechanismOn killing the query compiler

The single most consequential decision in the design of .tql is the one most likely to be misread as laziness: there is no query compiler. None. We deleted it. There is no central service that takes a "semantic query" and emits SQL. There is no intermediate representation. There is no opaque translation layer between the model and the warehouse. There is the file, which contains SQL fragments and the lambdas and helpers that splice them together, and there is the warehouse that runs the rendered SQL. That is the whole architecture.

This is heresy by the standards of the LookML lineage, where the compiler is the product. Looker's compiler turns LookML into SQL. Cube's compiler turns Cube schema into SQL. MetricFlow's compiler turns YAML-defined metrics into SQL. Malloy is essentially nothing but a compiler. OmniML's compiler is the moat the Series C deck talks about. The compiler is the thing customers are paying for, and it is also the thing nobody is allowed to look at.

A compiler nobody can inspect is a feature in approximately the same sense that a locked filing cabinet is a security feature.
Fig. 1 — Two architectures. The thing in the middle on the left is what every other semantic layer is selling you.

The case for the compiler used to be straightforward. You wanted to write your model once and have it run against Snowflake or BigQuery or Redshift or Postgres or Databricks without rewriting the joins. So the compiler took on the burden of dialect translation: window function syntax, date arithmetic, identifier quoting, the special way each warehouse spells QUALIFY. Multi-dialect support became a feature in every vendor's marketing. It is, in fact, a tax. The reason you need it is that you are not writing SQL anymore; you are writing a YAML description of SQL, and someone has to translate. If you just write SQL, the dialect problem ceases to exist as a product problem and reverts to being the customer's problem, which is the right place for it.8

Four things fall out of the decision once you actually make it.

Inspectability. An agent can reason about code it can read. An agent cannot reason about behavior generated by a compiler it has never seen the source of. This is the property we care about more than any other. Ana opens a .tql file and sees the SQL that will run. She can trace why a filter behaves a certain way by reading the file, not by running test queries against a black box and inferring the rules. The same is true of any future agent that touches the system. We are not the last AI vendor that will read these files; the format outlives us if it is legible.

Locality of behavior. The file shows what runs. Reviewing a PR to a .tql file is reviewing the SQL. There is no "and also, somewhere in the compiler, this gets transformed into X" footnote. The thing that runs is the thing in the file, modulo parameter substitution that you can also read in the file. This is what the TQL manual calls locality of behavior, and it is the single feature most likely to convince a skeptical data engineer to take the format seriously after about ninety seconds of reading.

Honest representation of imperfect warehouses. Real enterprise warehouses contain legacy tables with mixed grain, role-playing dimensions, bridge tables that need three-way joins to be correct, tenant scoping rules that depend on the calling user's claims, and exactly one materialized view that is wrong on Tuesdays for reasons nobody remembers. A semantic UI cannot model any of this without either lying about it or shipping a new compiler feature. A .tql file just writes the SQL, including the part that handles Tuesdays. The format does not pretend the warehouse is clean.

The tenant-scoping case is worth showing because it is the kind of logic that consumes whole product surfaces in conventional BI tools and lands as a few lines of plain code in a .tql file:

-- queries/accounts_scoped.tql
-- Fail-closed row-level scoping. If the caller's roles don't include
-- tenant access, the file refuses to render rather than emit unscoped SQL.

let
  tenant_id = _tql.client_attributes_json.tenant_id
  has_access = any (map (\role -> contains _tql.roleset_by_rolename role)
                    ["tenant_admin", "tenant_viewer"])
  scoped_tenant_id =
    if has_access then tenant_id
    else error "Query requires tenant_admin or tenant_viewer"
in sql''
  SELECT *
  FROM accounts
  WHERE tenant_id = ${scoped_tenant_id}
''

Two things are worth noticing. First, error is a primitive: it doesn't return a SQL fragment, it refuses to render the file at all. There is no path through this file's logic that emits SELECT-without-WHERE. The access check is not enforced by a separate authorization service that hopes you remembered to call it; it is enforced by the file failing to compose. Second, the caller's identity (tenant ID, role set) is available as ordinary values under the _tql namespace, so authorization logic lives in the same file as the SQL it gates, where a reviewer can see it. Try writing this in a semantic-UI tool. It is not impossible, exactly, but it requires touching three subsystems and trusting that all of them stay in sync.

A smaller bug surface, and one that lives in the file. Compilers are some of the most bug-rich software in the industry. When a compiler produces wrong SQL, the bug is in someone else's code and your fix is to wait. When a .tql file produces wrong SQL, the bug is in your file and your fix is to edit it. This is not a small thing in production.

The honest cost of this choice is the one the compiler was originally invented to handle: portability across warehouse dialects. If a customer runs both Snowflake and Databricks and wants identical semantics across both, they are going to fork some .tql files, or write a small helper module that branches on warehouse type. We have done this and it is not painful. The cost of forking a small text file is much smaller than the cost of maintaining a multi-dialect compiler, and the cost lands on the customer who actually has the multi-warehouse problem rather than on every customer who doesn't.

What is striking, in retrospect, is how often the compiler was the part of the product that was hardest to staff, hardest to test, hardest to extend, and hardest to explain to customers, and how often it was also the part of the product that was sold as the moat. We think it was actually a liability sold as a moat. We do not have one and the product is better for it.

VII — Worked contrastOne revenue vs. seventeen revenues

The philosophical inversion is easiest to see at the level of a single business term being asked about under both regimes.

LookML / OmniML / MetricFlow
.tql
One canonical revenue measure. Curated by data team. Six-week review cycle to change.
Seventeen .tql files that compute something called revenue. Created opportunistically, one per important thread.
If a sales rep asks "what's revenue for enterprise this quarter," they get the definition.
The agent reads all seventeen, notes that fourteen agree, picks the most-used one, and tells the rep which one it picked and why.
New definitions require a PR, a review, and a naming negotiation.
New definitions are created by the agent at the end of a user thread. No review required to exist; usage decides which ones matter.
Canonical meaning is decided by a governance committee.
Canonical meaning is whichever definition is referenced most often. Decided by frequency.
Reader: human analyst with a screen.
Reader: agent that can hold the whole repo in context.
If you write it twice, you have done it wrong, and a senior data engineer will tell you so in a pull request. The LookML worldview, in fifteen words

Neither regime is universally correct. The LookML regime fits when the cost of getting the wrong number is enormous and the cost of slow iteration is small, which describes regulatory reporting, the 10-K, and the board deck. The .tql regime fits when question volume is high, definitions evolve weekly, and the reader of the file is an agent that can be trusted to pick.

The interesting question is not which regime is right, but which fraction of analytical questions falls into each bucket. Our bet, and the bet behind every .tql file we have written, is that the agent-readable bucket is growing rapidly and the YAML-shaped semantic layers are not designed to serve it.

VIII — What we've seenReturns from leaning into redundancy

We have run this approach across a handful of enterprise deployments in financial services, healthcare, and one large industrial. A few patterns are consistent enough to mention without overselling.

Ontology repos grow faster than LookML projects, by roughly an order of magnitude in file count. The files are smaller, locally scoped, and most are authored by Ana (TextQL's analytical agent) at the close of a user thread rather than by a person. Many are never opened by a human; they are read mostly by other agents.

The definitions that get used the most stabilize quickly. Within a few weeks of a deployment, the agent can answer the question "what is this company's most-used definition of churn?" by counting references in the repo rather than asking a committee. That answer changes, sometimes monthly, and the system handles the change without a migration or a v2 metric: a new file simply gets used more often than the old one.

The traditional governance failure mode, where two teams have been using two different formulas for years without realizing it, does not disappear under this approach. It becomes legible. An agent can answer "is Sales computing revenue the same way Finance is?" by reading the files each team uses, and the answer is often no, which is useful to know.

This has been true at every company we have deployed at. The only variable is how confidently the CFO denies it in the kickoff meeting.
§ § §

IX — The betWhat we're actually claiming

The bet is small and specific. Frontier models have changed which reader the semantic layer is optimized for, and the LookML lineage was optimized for the previous reader. A semantic layer designed for agents should be redundancy-tolerant rather than DRY, frequency-canonical rather than committee-canonical, and shaped like markdown rather than YAML. .tql is our cut at that file format, and the returns so far have been larger than we expected.

We are probably wrong about some of the details. Closer to the truth: there will be a third or fourth iteration of this format, and the version of .tql in production a year from now will look meaningfully different from the version in production today. The direction holds. The file format is open, the manual lives in the ontology repo, and as of last week the agent has authored more .tql files than we have.

If this turns out to be wrong, we will write a graceful retrospective in 2030. Benn has a template.
